causes:
  - title: Customer's available balance is below the charge amount
    body: "The most literal cause — the card's account simply doesn't hold enough money. Note this is *available* balance, not posted balance: pending transactions, holds, and authorisations all reduce the spendable total even if the customer's app shows a higher number."
  - title: Daily or weekly spend cap on the card
    body: "Many debit cards (especially in the UK and EU) have configurable daily limits that the cardholder may not realise are set. The bank returns `insufficient_funds` even though the account has the money, because the limit is exhausted."
  - title: Pending authorisation hold consuming the balance
    body: "An earlier authorisation (hotel deposit, fuel pump pre-auth, subscription pre-charge) is still holding funds. Stripe's charge can't go through until the prior auth is captured or released, which can take 7-30 days for some merchants."
  - title: Currency conversion buffer leaving customer short
    body: "When the card is denominated in a different currency to the charge, the issuer reserves a buffer (usually 5-10%) on top of the converted amount to absorb FX moves. A customer with £100 may fail a £95 charge in EUR if the buffer pushes the reservation over £100."
  - title: Prepaid or gift card with no top-up
    body: "Prepaid cards return `insufficient_funds` once the balance is spent. Unlike a regular debit card, they can't be auto-topped-up, so retrying is pointless until the customer reloads."

faq:
  - q: "Is `insufficient_funds` a hard decline or a soft decline?"
    a: "Soft. The card itself is valid and active — the customer just doesn't have enough balance right now. They can retry the same card later once funds are available. This is unlike hard declines (`lost_card`, `stolen_card`, `pickup_card`) where the card is permanently blocked."
  - q: "Should I auto-retry an `insufficient_funds` decline?"
    a: "Not for one-off purchases — wait for the customer to initiate a retry. For subscription invoices, enable Stripe Billing Smart Retries which learns optimal retry timing. Auto-retrying one-off payments without consent risks customer complaints and chargebacks."
  - q: "Why does `insufficient_funds` happen even when the customer says they have money?"
    a: "Available balance ≠ posted balance. Pending authorisations (hotel deposits, fuel pump pre-auths, subscription holds) reduce spendable funds even though the customer's banking app may still show the higher figure. Currency conversion buffers and daily spend caps also commonly trigger it."
  - q: "Does `insufficient_funds` count toward my Stripe decline rate?"
    a: "Yes. Stripe tracks all `card_declined` decline codes (including `insufficient_funds`) in your decline rate. A sustained high decline rate can attract Stripe Risk review, even though `insufficient_funds` itself is benign and not fraud-related."
  - q: "Can I see how often customers retry successfully after `insufficient_funds`?"
    a: "Yes — link your retry attempts via `metadata.original_payment_intent` and query Stripe Sigma or your own logs. Industry average for customer-initiated retry success on `insufficient_funds` is 30-45% within 7 days, much lower after that."
  - q: "Is `insufficient_funds` ever a fraud indicator?"
    a: "Rarely. Fraudsters typically hit `do_not_honor` or `pickup_card` because stolen cards get flagged or run dry from prior fraud spend. `insufficient_funds` from a long-time customer is almost always genuinely about their balance."
  - q: "How does `insufficient_funds` interact with 3D Secure?"
    a: "3DS authenticates the cardholder; it doesn't check funds. A card can pass 3DS authentication and still decline as `insufficient_funds` immediately afterwards. Always handle the decline path even when 3DS succeeded."
  - q: "What's the difference between `insufficient_funds` from Stripe and from a bank statement?"
    a: "Same root cause — issuer says no funds available — but Stripe normalises the wire-protocol decline reason (ISO 8583 code 51) into the friendly string `insufficient_funds`. Bank statements may show 'NSF' (non-sufficient funds) for the same event."

`insufficient_funds` is the most common reason a Stripe `card_declined` error fires. It means the issuing bank checked the customer's available balance, found it less than the amount you tried to charge, and refused authorisation. Stripe didn't decline — the bank did. Your job is to surface the right message and let the customer recover gracefully without losing the sale.

The trick with `insufficient_funds` is that it lies somewhere between fraud declines (where you should never retry) and processing errors (where you should retry quickly). Treat it as a temporary state on a valid card: tell the customer clearly, offer an alternative payment method on the same screen, and if they retry on their own initiative later, let them.
